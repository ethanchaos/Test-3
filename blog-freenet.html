Freenet & Free Net Technology Explained

What "free net" means in the real world — from Ian Clarke's groundbreaking Freenet software to the broader movement of censorship-resistant, open-access internet technology. What it is, how it works, and why it matters now more than ever.

📖 ~7,000 words ⏱️ 28 min read 🏷️ Beginner · Foundational

What Is "Free Net Technology"?

The term "free net technology" encompasses a broad and evolving set of tools, protocols, platforms, and philosophies united by a single core idea: that access to information and communication over the internet should be free — free as in uncensored, free as in open, and in many contexts, free as in zero cost for people who can't afford otherwise.

Free net technology isn't a single product or protocol. It's a category — and understanding it requires looking at several distinct but related areas:

  1. Freenet the software — Ian Clarke's original peer-to-peer censorship-resistant network, published in 2000 and still actively developed
  2. Censorship circumvention tools — VPNs, proxies, Tor, I2P, and the suite of tunneling protocols used to bypass restrictions
  3. Zero-cost internet access technology — the configs, apps, and techniques that allow users to access the internet in environments where conventional data access is unaffordable or restricted
  4. Decentralized communication platforms — systems designed to operate without centralized infrastructure that can be shut down or censored

At Chaos Configurations, when we talk about free net technology, we mean all of these things — and we approach them both technically (how do they work?) and practically (how do you use them?). Understanding the full spectrum is essential to understanding why this work matters and what it's trying to achieve.

Is Free Net Technology Legal?

This is one of the most commonly asked questions, and the honest answer is: it depends on where you are and what you're doing with it.

In most democratic countries — including the United States, most of Europe, Canada, Australia, and many others — using VPN software, Freenet, Tor, and related privacy technologies is entirely legal. These tools are used daily by journalists, privacy-conscious individuals, remote workers, and security researchers for completely legitimate purposes. There is no law against encrypting your internet traffic or routing it through a VPN.

The situation is different in some countries:

  • China — VPNs are heavily regulated. Unauthorized VPN use (i.e., using a VPN not approved by the government) is technically illegal for individuals, though enforcement is inconsistent. Businesses can use government-approved VPNs. Tor is blocked at the network level.
  • Russia — VPNs must register with Roskomnadzor (the government communications regulator) and block access to sites on the government blacklist. Many VPN providers have exited the Russian market rather than comply. Using a non-compliant VPN is technically illegal.
  • UAE, Oman, Qatar — VPN use for illegal activities is explicitly prohibited. The legality of general VPN use is murky; using a VPN to access VOIP services (which are blocked for commercial reasons) is specifically illegal.
  • Belarus, North Korea, Turkmenistan — Among the most restrictive; essentially all circumvention tools are illegal.

For zero-cost internet access techniques (using carrier loopholes, zero-rated SNI configs, etc.): this falls into a gray area in most jurisdictions. Most carriers' Terms of Service prohibit using their zero-rated services to tunnel other traffic — so it may violate carrier ToS even if it doesn't violate any law. The legal situation varies by country and specific technique.

The educational content on Chaos Configurations — explaining how these technologies work, their history, and the context around them — is legal everywhere.

Freenet — The Original Free Net Software

Freenet is a peer-to-peer platform for censorship-resistant, anonymous communication. It was originally designed by Ian Clarke as his final-year project at the University of Edinburgh in 1999, and publicly released in March 2000. It is one of the most important pieces of internet freedom software ever created, and its influence can be seen in virtually every major anonymity and censorship-circumvention system that followed.

Freenet's core innovation was this: instead of storing documents on specific servers that can be located and shut down, it distributes encrypted fragments of data across thousands of participating nodes (computers running the Freenet software). No single node holds any complete document, and no node necessarily knows what it's storing — the data is encrypted. The network routes requests intelligently without any central index or directory.

How Freenet Works

When you publish a document on Freenet, it's broken into encrypted blocks and distributed across multiple nodes in the network. The blocks are stored based on keys derived from the content itself (content-addressed storage). When someone requests the document, the network routes the request through multiple intermediate nodes until it finds the blocks — without any node knowing who requested the document or who published it.

The routing algorithm is designed so that popular content automatically migrates toward the nodes where it's most frequently requested — improving performance without any central coordination. Documents that are rarely accessed may eventually be dropped from the network to make room for more popular content (though this can be counteracted by re-publishing).

There are two modes of operation:

  • Opennet mode — Your node connects to other random nodes in the network. Easier to set up, provides reasonable anonymity, but theoretically more vulnerable to traffic analysis because your connections are publicly discoverable.
  • Darknet mode — Your node only connects to nodes run by people you personally trust. Much more resistant to surveillance and infiltration, but requires knowing other Freenet users. A global darknet (where users only connect to friends of friends) is theoretically resistant to nation-state-level attacks.

What Can You Do on Freenet?

Freenet supports several types of applications and content:

  • Freesites — Static websites hosted entirely on the Freenet network. They're slow to load compared to regular websites (because of the routing overhead), but they're essentially impossible to take down.
  • Forums and message boards — Decentralized discussion systems like FMS (Freenet Message System) that allow anonymous communication without central servers.
  • File sharing — Sharing any type of file, with the same censorship-resistant, anonymous properties as everything else on Freenet.
  • River — A new decentralized group chat application launched in 2026 as part of Freenet's next-generation network redesign.

Freenet vs. Hyphanet

In 2023, the Freenet Project made a significant decision that caused some confusion: the original Freenet software was renamed Hyphanet, and the "Freenet" name was given to a new, redesigned next-generation network. The new Freenet (as of 2026) uses a WebAssembly-based contract system and is focused on real-time decentralized applications, while Hyphanet continues to maintain the original distributed data store approach.

For most practical purposes and in common usage, when people say "Freenet," they're referring to the original system and its philosophy — whether they call it Freenet or Hyphanet today.

Tor (The Onion Router)

Tor routes your traffic through a series of volunteer-operated relays (nodes), encrypting it at each hop so that no single node knows both who you are and what you're doing. The three-relay "circuit" (entry guard → middle relay → exit relay) provides anonymity against most adversaries.

Tor is primarily designed for anonymous access to the regular internet. You can browse normal websites through Tor, with the exit relay appearing to be your IP address. Tor also hosts ".onion" hidden services — websites that exist entirely within the Tor network and can't be accessed from the regular internet.

I2P (Invisible Internet Project)

I2P is designed as a private, encrypted internet in its own right — less focused on accessing the regular web and more focused on communication between I2P users. It uses a "garlic routing" protocol (a variation on Tor's onion routing) and is particularly suited to peer-to-peer applications, torrenting, and running services within the I2P network.

VPNs and Tunneling as Free Net Technology

While Freenet, Tor, and I2P represent the academic and technical side of free net technology, the practical free net landscape in much of the world is dominated by a different set of tools: VPN configurations and tunneling applications.

In regions like Sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and parts of the Middle East, the dominant challenge isn't necessarily government censorship (though that exists too) — it's the cost of data. Mobile data is expensive relative to local incomes, and many people simply can't afford unrestricted internet access. This is where the carrier-based free net techniques that are central to Chaos Configurations' work come in.

Apps like HTTP Injector and HA Tunnel Plus allow users to leverage carrier network characteristics — zero-rated domains, misconfigured proxies, open ports — to access the internet without consuming their data balance. The configurations for these apps require understanding how carrier networks work, what domains they whitelist, and how to structure tunnel requests to appear as whitelisted traffic.

This is practical free net technology in action. It's not as philosophically pure as Freenet's censorship-resistant distributed storage — but it solves a real problem that millions of people face every day.

Free Net Technology and Digital Equity

The deepest reason free net technology matters is this: access to information is not equally distributed. In wealthy countries, most people have affordable, reliable, high-speed internet access. They can Google anything, read any news source, communicate with anyone in the world, access education platforms, run businesses online.

In many developing countries, the picture is dramatically different. Data is expensive. Networks are slow. Many people access the internet only through mobile phones — and only when they can afford to buy data. When the data runs out, the internet stops. For these users, free net technology isn't a luxury or a privacy preference — it's the difference between having access to the information economy and being locked out of it.

This is why Ethan Chaos and Chaos Configurations approach free net technology not just as a technical subject but as a matter of digital equity. The work of building, testing, and distributing VPN configs and tunneling tools is, in a meaningful sense, the work of expanding access to the open internet for people who would otherwise go without.
