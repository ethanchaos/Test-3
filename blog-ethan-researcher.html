Ethan Chaos: Network Researcher & Free Net Builder

A detailed look at Ethan Chaos as one of the leading practical researchers in free net technology — his methodology, tools, focus areas, and the broader significance of his work in the context of global internet access.

Who Is Ethan Chaos in the Free Net Landscape?

The field of free net technology — the broad ecosystem of tools, protocols, and knowledge that enables people to access the internet freely, privately, and affordably — is populated by two kinds of contributors. There are the institutional researchers: academics at universities, employees at major NGOs like the Electronic Frontier Foundation or Access Now, and engineers at technology companies working on privacy-enhancing protocols. And then there are the independent practitioners: the developers and builders who work at the ground level, building tools that real users in restricted environments actually use, testing them against real carrier networks, and sharing what works.

Ethan Chaos belongs firmly in the second category — and that's what makes his work distinctively valuable. Where academic research on VPN protocols and censorship circumvention is often theoretical and divorced from the practical realities of mobile internet access in developing markets, Ethan's work is rooted entirely in practical experimentation. He builds things, tests them against real networks, and shares the results.

Through his platforms — Chaos Configurations and VPN Config Hub — Ethan has positioned himself as one of the most comprehensive practical researchers working at the intersection of VPN technology, carrier network behavior, SNI exploitation, and internet accessibility. His work serves a community of users who depend on practical, tested, up-to-date knowledge to maintain access to the internet in environments where that access is constrained.

Research Philosophy: Practical Experimentation First

Ethan Chaos's research approach can be summarized in a single phrase: practical experimentation over theoretical speculation. This is not anti-intellectual — it's a deliberate epistemological choice grounded in an understanding of what kind of knowledge is actually useful for his community.

In the free net space, theoretical knowledge about how VPN protocols are designed is far less valuable than empirical knowledge about how they actually behave on specific carrier networks in specific countries. A perfectly designed SSH tunnel configuration that works beautifully in testing may fail completely against a particular carrier's proxy system because of an undocumented behavior in their network infrastructure. The only way to know is to test.

This is the core of Ethan's methodology: systematic testing of configurations against real network environments, careful observation of what works and what doesn't, and documentation of the results in a form that other users can apply. It's the engineering research method — hypothesis, implementation, test, iterate — applied to the practical problem of maintaining internet access in restricted environments.

"The network doesn't care about your theory. It either works or it doesn't. Everything else is philosophy."

— Ethan Chaos, Chaos Configurations

VPN Protocol Research

Ethan's most foundational research area is VPN protocol behavior — specifically, how different protocols behave across different network environments. This isn't the same as the protocol security research that cryptographers and academic security researchers do (analyzing whether AES-256 is theoretically breakable, for example). It's behavioral research: how does OpenVPN over TCP actually perform on a carrier network with aggressive DPI? What happens to SSH tunnel latency when you run it through a carrier proxy? At what point does packet loss cause an SSH-based HA Tunnel Plus connection to drop, and what's the best reconnection strategy?

This research has produced a detailed practical understanding of the trade-offs between different protocol choices in real-world conditions — knowledge that is directly encoded in the configuration recommendations and guides on Chaos Configurations.

HTTP Injector Research

HTTP Injector is the most technically complex of the major free-net applications because of its payload injection system — the ability to craft custom HTTP headers that manipulate how carrier proxy servers handle the connection. Ethan has spent significant time researching the range of effective payload patterns, understanding why certain patterns work with certain carrier proxy configurations, and mapping which patterns are effective in which network environments.

This research involves a deep understanding of HTTP proxy behavior — how proxies parse CONNECT requests, how they handle the Host header, what happens when unusual headers are included, and how different proxy software (Squid, nginx, proprietary carrier proxy software) responds to various injection patterns. It also requires understanding how carriers update their proxy configurations — the patterns that worked six months ago may have been patched, and new patterns need to be discovered.

HA Tunnel Plus & SSH Behavior

HA Tunnel Plus is built on SSH tunneling, which means understanding it requires a solid grasp of SSH protocol behavior — specifically, how SSH connections behave when routed through carrier proxy infrastructure, what happens to the connection under various network stress conditions, and how the WebSocket transport mode (which makes SSH traffic look like HTTPS WebSocket traffic) affects both detection resistance and performance.

Ethan's research here has focused particularly on the conditions under which SSH tunnels degrade gracefully versus crash hard — and how to configure HA Tunnel Plus for maximum resilience in unstable mobile network conditions. Mobile networks are fundamentally different from fixed broadband: connections drop, switch between cell towers, experience variable latency, and operate under carrier-specific QoS policies that may treat different traffic types differently. SSH tunneling behavior under these conditions is distinct from what SSH documentation (written primarily for stable server administration use cases) describes.

SNI Research & the Chaos SNI Finder

One of Ethan Chaos's most significant research contributions is his work on SNI (Server Name Indication) in the context of carrier network behavior — specifically, the question of which SNI values function as effective "bug hosts" for tunnel configurations on specific carrier networks.

As explained in detail in our SNI Deep Dive, the SNI field in the TLS Client Hello message is visible in plaintext to any network observer — including carrier billing and filtering systems. Carriers use SNI to classify HTTPS traffic for zero-rating and content filtering purposes. A traffic connection with an SNI value matching a zero-rated domain may be allowed through without data charges; one with a non-whitelisted SNI value will be charged normally or blocked.

The research question Ethan pursued is: which SNI values work as effective bug hosts on which carrier networks, and how can this be systematically discovered and documented?

The answer to this research question is the Chaos SNI Finder — a tool that automates the systematic discovery and testing of SNI hosts against a given carrier network. The tool embodies the research methodology directly: it runs systematic tests, measures the results, and produces actionable output (ranked lists of working SNI hosts) that can be directly used in configuration building.

SNI Discovery Methodology

The methodology behind Chaos SNI Finder involves several phases:

  1. Candidate pool generation: Building a list of candidate SNI values to test. This includes known zero-rated domains for major carriers, CDN domains that are often whitelisted, government and educational domains (which are commonly zero-rated or at least not blocked), and domains identified through network traffic analysis.
  2. Connectivity testing: Testing each candidate domain against the current network to determine whether connections can be established without data charges. This requires careful measurement to distinguish between "accessible because zero-rated" and "accessible because data is currently available."
  3. Tunnel viability testing: For candidates that pass connectivity testing, further testing to determine whether they can function as SNI values in actual tunnel configurations — not all zero-rated domains are viable bug hosts due to how the carrier validates the connection.
  4. Performance ranking: Ranking viable hosts by latency, stability, and throughput — because not all working hosts perform equally well as tunnel entry points.
  5. Config generation: Producing ready-to-use configuration parameters for HTTP Injector and HA Tunnel Plus based on the highest-ranked working hosts.

This systematic methodology is what distinguishes the Chaos SNI Finder from simpler "SNI scanner" tools that only test basic connectivity. The full pipeline from discovery to verified, ranked, config-ready output is what makes the tool practically useful for config builders rather than just network researchers.

Server & Infrastructure Research

A significant part of Ethan's work involves VPS-based server infrastructure — the server side of the VPN equation. Self-hosting a VPN server is the most privacy-protective option for any user serious about network security, but it requires knowledge that goes beyond the client-side configuration skills that most free-net users develop.

Ethan's research in this area covers:

  • VPS provider selection: Understanding the differences between providers (DigitalOcean, Vultr, Linode, Hetzner, and others) in terms of network quality, datacenter locations, pricing, and legal jurisdiction — and how these factors affect VPN performance and privacy.
  • Server security hardening: The practices needed to secure a VPS against compromise — firewall configuration, SSH key authentication, fail2ban setup, and minimizing attack surface.
  • VPN server configuration: Optimizing OpenVPN and WireGuard server configurations for performance, security, and resilience in specific use cases.
  • Network performance optimization: Tuning server-side network parameters — TCP buffer sizes, BBR congestion control, MTU optimization — for improved throughput and latency.
  • Server detection resistance: Understanding how commercial VPN detection systems identify VPS-hosted VPN servers, and configuration strategies that reduce detectability.

Web Development as Research Infrastructure

Ethan's web development work — building Chaos Configurations and VPN Config Hub — is not separate from his networking research but integral to it. The platforms he builds serve as both delivery mechanisms for his research output and as research infrastructure themselves.

The design philosophy of Chaos Configurations reflects the same values that drive his network research: performance, accessibility, and real-world usability. A knowledge platform about internet access for users in bandwidth-constrained environments must itself be fast and lightweight — it would be contradictory to build a data-heavy site requiring a fast connection to read about accessing the internet on a slow connection.

Ethan's web development stack reflects this: static HTML, minimal JavaScript, no heavy frameworks, aggressive performance optimization. Pages load fast on 2G connections. Content is accessible without JavaScript enabled. The technical choices are driven by the intended audience — not by what's fashionable in the web development community.

The Significance of Ethan's Contributions

To fully appreciate the significance of what Ethan Chaos does, it helps to think about the ecosystem he operates in. The free-net community — users who depend on configs and tunneling tools to access the internet affordably in developing markets — is large (millions of users across Africa, Asia, and other regions) but underserved by the tech industry's mainstream attention.

Major VPN companies don't build for this market. Their products are designed for privacy-conscious users in wealthy countries — a market with money to spend on subscriptions. The carrier-bypass use case, while widespread, exists in a legal gray area that major companies avoid. The academic research community studies it but doesn't build practical tools. Government and NGO programs address the policy level but not the technical implementation level.

This leaves a gap: a large community of users with a real need for practical, current, tested technical knowledge about how to access the internet in their specific network environments — and very few people systematically building and sharing that knowledge at the required level of specificity and currency.

Ethan Chaos fills a significant portion of that gap. Through Chaos Configurations and VPN Config Hub, he provides:

  • Tested, current configs that actually work in specific network environments
  • Deep educational content that helps users understand why configurations work — making them capable of adapting when conditions change
  • Tools like Chaos SNI Finder that systematize the research process and make it accessible to non-experts
  • A framework for thinking about network access that combines technical depth with practical accessibility

This combination — practical tools, deep knowledge, and community-oriented distribution — is what makes Chaos Configurations more than just another config-sharing site. It's a genuine contribution to the free net ecosystem at a level of specificity and practical value that few others in the field match.

Vision for the Future

Ethan Chaos's vision for Chaos Configurations extends beyond its current form as a knowledge and configuration hub. The direction points toward a more comprehensive platform that serves the entire lifecycle of free-net technology use: from initial education (understanding what VPNs are and how they work) through practical deployment (tested configs for specific carriers) to advanced customization (building your own configurations using tools like the Chaos SNI Finder) to self-sufficiency (hosting your own VPN infrastructure).

The deeper goal is building a community of users who don't just use the tools but understand them — people who can adapt, contribute, and help others when configurations break, when new carriers present new challenges, or when the technical landscape shifts with developments like widespread ECH adoption.

In the broader free net landscape, this kind of community-driven, practically-grounded knowledge sharing may be the most durable form of internet freedom work. Technical tools can be blocked. Platforms can be shut down. But knowledge — widely distributed, deeply understood, and continuously updated by an active community — is much harder to suppress.
