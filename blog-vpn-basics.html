VPN Basics: The Complete Guide

What a VPN is, how encryption and tunneling actually work, the difference between TCP and UDP, and why any of this matters — explained clearly from first principles.

📖 ~5,500 words ⏱️ 22 min read 🏷️ Beginner · Intermediate

What Is a VPN?

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) is a technology that creates a secure, encrypted connection between your device and a remote server — and routes your internet traffic through that connection. From the perspective of websites, services, and anyone monitoring the network, your traffic appears to originate from the VPN server rather than your actual device and location.

The word "virtual" is important here. A VPN doesn't create a new physical network — it creates a logical network that runs on top of the existing public internet. Your data travels through the same cables and routers as everyone else's, but it's wrapped in an encrypted tunnel that makes it unreadable to anyone who intercepts it along the way.

To understand why this matters, you need to understand what happens to your internet traffic without a VPN. Every time you visit a website, your device sends requests that pass through your Internet Service Provider (ISP), their upstream network providers, various routers and switching nodes, and eventually reach the destination server. At every one of those points, your traffic can potentially be observed, logged, or interfered with. Your ISP can see every domain you visit. Government surveillance systems can intercept and log your requests. A malicious actor on the same Wi-Fi network can potentially read unencrypted data you're sending.

A VPN addresses this by encrypting your traffic at the source and routing it through a server you control (or trust). Your ISP can see that you're connected to a VPN server — but they can't see what you're actually doing once connected. The destination servers see requests coming from the VPN server's IP address, not yours.

How VPN Encryption Works

Encryption is the process of transforming readable data (plaintext) into an unreadable scrambled format (ciphertext) using a mathematical algorithm and a key. Only someone with the correct decryption key can reverse the process and read the original data.

Modern VPN protocols use symmetric encryption for the actual data transfer — meaning both sides use the same key to encrypt and decrypt. The most common algorithm used today is AES-256 (Advanced Encryption Standard with a 256-bit key). AES-256 is essentially unbreakable with current computing technology — a brute-force attack against it would take longer than the current age of the universe.

But here's the challenge: if both sides need the same key, how do they agree on that key without someone intercepting it during the initial exchange? This is solved through asymmetric key exchange, typically using algorithms like RSA, Diffie-Hellman (DH), or Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH). These allow two parties to establish a shared secret key over an insecure channel without ever actually sending the key itself — only mathematical values that together produce the same result on both ends.

The TLS Handshake

Most modern VPN protocols (and HTTPS, which you use every time you visit a secure website) establish encryption through a process called the TLS handshake. Here's what happens:

  1. Client Hello: Your device sends a message to the server announcing which TLS version and cipher suites it supports. This message also includes a random number.
  2. Server Hello: The server responds with its chosen cipher suite, its own random number, and its digital certificate (which contains the server's public key).
  3. Certificate verification: Your device verifies the server's certificate against a trusted Certificate Authority (CA). This confirms you're talking to the real server and not an impostor.
  4. Key exchange: Using the server's public key and the agreed algorithm, both sides perform a key exchange that produces a shared session key — without ever transmitting the key itself.
  5. Session established: All subsequent communication is encrypted with the shared session key using symmetric encryption (like AES-256).

This process happens in milliseconds every time you make a secure connection. Understanding it is fundamental to understanding how VPN protocols — and SNI, which we cover in depth elsewhere — actually work.

Forward Secrecy

A critical concept in modern encryption is Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS). With PFS, each session uses a unique session key that is generated fresh and discarded after use. This means that even if an attacker somehow obtained your long-term private key in the future, they couldn't use it to decrypt past sessions — because those sessions used different, temporary keys that no longer exist.

PFS is implemented through ephemeral key exchange algorithms — typically ECDHE (Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman Ephemeral). Good VPN implementations always use PFS. If you're evaluating a VPN provider or configuring your own server, PFS support is non-negotiable.

VPN Tunneling Protocols

A tunneling protocol defines the rules for how your data is encapsulated, encrypted, and transmitted through the VPN. Think of it as the specific language and format used to create the secure channel. Different protocols make different trade-offs between security, speed, compatibility, and the ability to evade detection.

OpenVPN

OpenVPN is the most widely used open-source VPN protocol and the foundation of much of Ethan Chaos's work on Chaos Configurations. It runs on top of TLS/SSL, which makes it extremely flexible — it can run on any port, including port 443, which is the standard HTTPS port. This matters enormously for bypassing firewalls: networks that block all VPN traffic often still allow port 443 because blocking it would break all HTTPS traffic, which would make the network unusable for most purposes.

OpenVPN can run over either TCP or UDP (explained in detail below). It supports a wide range of cipher suites and authentication methods, making it highly configurable. The downside is that it's more computationally intensive than newer protocols and can be slower as a result. It's also somewhat detectable to sophisticated Deep Packet Inspection (DPI) systems because its TLS patterns have identifiable characteristics.

Despite these limitations, OpenVPN remains the gold standard for reliability and compatibility. It works on virtually every platform, has been extensively audited by the security community, and has a massive ecosystem of tools and documentation around it.

WireGuard

WireGuard is a modern VPN protocol that has taken the networking world by storm since its first stable release. Where OpenVPN is built on decades-old SSL/TLS infrastructure, WireGuard was designed from scratch as a kernel-level VPN implementation. The entire codebase is roughly 4,000 lines — compared to hundreds of thousands for OpenVPN. This minimal codebase means fewer potential vulnerabilities and much easier security auditing.

WireGuard is significantly faster than OpenVPN in most environments, particularly for handshake times and reconnection after network changes (important for mobile users who frequently switch between WiFi and cellular data). It uses modern cryptographic algorithms (ChaCha20, Poly1305, Curve25519, BLAKE2s, SipHash24) that are both fast and secure.

The main limitation of WireGuard for the free-net use case is that it uses UDP exclusively and has a distinctive network fingerprint, making it easier to detect and block with DPI. For users in heavily restricted environments, obfuscated protocols may be more appropriate.

SSH Tunneling

Secure Shell (SSH) was originally designed as a secure replacement for Telnet — a way to remotely access and manage servers over an encrypted connection. But SSH also supports tunneling — the ability to route arbitrary TCP traffic through the encrypted SSH connection.

SSH tunneling is widely used in the free-net community, particularly in applications like HA Tunnel Plus. The advantage is that SSH traffic is extremely common and legitimate — millions of developers and system administrators use SSH every day to manage servers. Traffic that looks like SSH is therefore much less likely to be blocked than traffic that looks like a VPN connection.

SSH uses port 22 by default, but can be configured to run on any port, including 443 (though running SSH on 443 can be detected by the fact that it's SSH rather than TLS). For maximum stealth, SSH tunneling is often combined with additional obfuscation techniques.

SSL/TLS Tunneling

SSL/TLS tunneling wraps VPN traffic inside a standard SSL/TLS connection — making it look identical to normal HTTPS traffic to any observer who doesn't have the decryption keys. This is the basis for Stunnel, which is used as a wrapper around OpenVPN and other protocols to make them appear as regular HTTPS traffic.

Because modern HTTPS traffic is TLS-encrypted and the destination information is protected (especially with newer protocols like Encrypted Client Hello), SSL-tunneled VPN traffic is extremely difficult to detect and block without causing significant collateral damage to normal web browsing. HTTP Injector configurations often leverage SSL tunneling for exactly this reason.

V2Ray / VMess / VLESS

V2Ray is a more advanced platform for building proxies and tunnels, offering a wide range of transport protocols and obfuscation options. VMess and VLESS are the core protocols in the V2Ray ecosystem — VLESS being the newer, lighter version without built-in encryption (relying instead on TLS for the transport layer).

V2Ray configurations can disguise traffic as WebSocket connections, HTTP/2 traffic, or even regular HTTP requests — making them exceptionally resistant to detection. The ecosystem is particularly popular in regions with sophisticated censorship systems like China's Great Firewall, where conventional VPN protocols are systematically blocked.

TCP vs UDP: A Critical Distinction

One of the most important configuration decisions when setting up or using a VPN is whether to run it over TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) or UDP (User Datagram Protocol). Understanding the difference between these two transport protocols is fundamental to understanding why different configurations behave differently in different network environments.

TCP: Reliable but Slower

TCP is the reliable workhorse of the internet. It provides guaranteed delivery — every packet sent over a TCP connection is acknowledged by the receiver, and any lost packets are automatically retransmitted. TCP also guarantees that packets are delivered in order — if packet 5 arrives before packet 4, TCP buffers packet 5 and waits for packet 4 before delivering both to the application.

This reliability comes at a cost. TCP requires a three-way handshake to establish a connection (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK), and the acknowledgment mechanism adds overhead to every connection. The connection-oriented nature of TCP also means it handles reconnection poorly — if your network changes while a TCP VPN connection is active (e.g., you switch from WiFi to cellular), the connection will typically drop and need to be re-established.

There's also a phenomenon called TCP-over-TCP meltdown — a serious performance problem that occurs when you run TCP traffic inside a TCP VPN tunnel. If the inner TCP connection experiences packet loss, both the inner and outer TCP layers will independently try to retransmit, causing exponential congestion that can make the connection unusable. For this reason, running a TCP-based VPN (like OpenVPN over TCP) is generally recommended only when UDP is specifically blocked by the network.

UDP: Fast but Unreliable

UDP is the opposite of TCP: it provides no delivery guarantees, no ordering, and no connection state. Packets are sent and forgotten — if they don't arrive, nobody retransmits them. This sounds like a terrible idea, but for many applications, it's actually superior to TCP.

VPN protocols that run over UDP — WireGuard, OpenVPN-UDP, most gaming VPNs — are generally significantly faster than their TCP equivalents. Because there's no acknowledgment overhead, packets move faster. Because there's no connection state, reconnection after a network change is nearly instantaneous. And because the VPN protocol itself handles any retransmission needs for its own control messages, the lack of UDP reliability doesn't cause the same problems as running TCP inside TCP.

The downside: some networks block UDP traffic aggressively, particularly on ports other than 53 (DNS) and 443 (HTTPS-over-QUIC/HTTP3). In these environments, UDP-based VPN protocols may not work at all, and you'll need to fall back to TCP-based configurations.

Ports, Protocols, and Firewall Evasion

Network firewalls work by inspecting traffic and deciding whether to allow or block it based on rules. These rules can be based on:

  • Port numbers — blocking specific destination ports (e.g., blocking port 1194 to prevent OpenVPN connections)
  • IP addresses — blocking connections to known VPN server IP ranges
  • Protocol signatures — using Deep Packet Inspection to identify VPN traffic patterns, even if it's on a non-standard port
  • SNI inspection — reading the Server Name Indication field in TLS handshakes to see what domain is being accessed (covered in depth in our SNI guide)

Understanding what type of blocking is in place determines which approach will work to bypass it. Port-based blocking is the simplest and most common — and it's defeated simply by running your VPN on a different port. Running OpenVPN on port 443 defeats most basic port-based firewalls because blocking port 443 breaks all HTTPS traffic.

IP-based blocking is more sophisticated but still has limitations. Commercial VPN providers often have their IP ranges blacklisted. This is one of the strongest arguments for self-hosting your VPN on a fresh VPS — the IP address of your server is unlikely to be pre-blocked.

DPI-based blocking is the most sophisticated and requires the most sophisticated countermeasures: traffic obfuscation that makes VPN traffic look like regular HTTPS or other innocent traffic types.

DNS Leaks and Why They Matter

A DNS leak is one of the most common ways a VPN can fail to protect your privacy, even when the VPN connection itself is working correctly. To understand DNS leaks, you need to understand what DNS is and how it interacts with your VPN connection.

DNS (Domain Name System) is the internet's phone book — it translates human-readable domain names (like "google.com") into the numerical IP addresses that computers actually use to route traffic. Every time you visit a website, your device first makes a DNS query to look up the IP address of the domain.

By default, your DNS queries go to DNS servers operated by your ISP. Even when you're connected to a VPN and your browsing traffic is encrypted and routed through the VPN, your DNS queries may still be going directly to your ISP's DNS servers — bypassing the VPN entirely. This means your ISP can see exactly which domains you're looking up, even though they can't see the content of your encrypted browsing sessions.

Preventing DNS leaks requires ensuring that all DNS queries are routed through the VPN tunnel, ideally to a DNS server that you control or that has a strong privacy policy. This is configurable in most VPN clients and is an important consideration when setting up self-hosted VPN infrastructure.

Kill Switches and Connection Drops

A kill switch is a VPN safety feature that monitors your VPN connection and automatically cuts off your device's internet access if the VPN connection drops. Without a kill switch, a dropped VPN connection results in your traffic reverting to your regular unencrypted connection — potentially exposing your activity to your ISP or network observers without any warning.

Kill switches are implemented either at the application level (the VPN client monitors its own connection and blocks traffic when disconnected) or at the OS/firewall level (network rules are configured so that traffic outside the VPN tunnel is simply blocked). OS-level kill switches are generally more reliable because they continue to work even if the VPN application crashes.

For users in environments where the reason they're using a VPN is specifically to avoid surveillance or censorship, a reliable kill switch is not optional — it's essential.

Choosing the Right Protocol for Your Environment

With all of these options, how do you choose the right protocol? The answer depends on your specific situation:

  • Open network, maximum speed: WireGuard over UDP is almost always the fastest and simplest option. If there's no censorship or restriction concern, WireGuard is the best choice.
  • Basic firewall/port blocking: OpenVPN over TCP on port 443 will bypass most simple firewalls. Pair with a fresh VPS IP for best results.
  • ISP-level restrictions, mobile carrier: SSH tunneling via HA Tunnel Plus or HTTP Injector with a crafted config that leverages zero-rated or whitelisted domains as the tunnel entry point.
  • Sophisticated DPI blocking: Obfuscated protocols like Shadowsocks, V2Ray, or obfs4-wrapped OpenVPN. SSL tunneling via Stunnel.
  • Maximum privacy, no performance concern: OpenVPN over TCP on port 443 with PFS, a no-logs server you control, full DNS-over-VPN, and an OS-level kill switch.
